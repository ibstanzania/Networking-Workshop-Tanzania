Invited Speaker's

Dr. Justine Nasejje. is a Researcher at the University of Witwatersrand in South Africa. Her expertise ranges from machine learning, underfive mortality, survival analysis, maternal deaths, and competing risks. She will share her expertise in deep learning and survival analysis.
Dr. Edmund Njeru Njagi is Associate Professor in Quantitative Analysis of Breast Cancer Screening at the Warwick Medical School — University of Warwick in the UK. With a background in Mathematics, Njeru obtained my Master's and PhD in Biostatistics at Hasselt University in Belgium. He specialised in analysis of complex routine health data in cancer epidemiology and taught Medical Statistics at the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine. He sits on the UK's National Health Service Race and Health Observatory's Academic Reference Group and serves as Faculty for the international biennial Corsican Summer School on Modern Methods in Biostatistics and Epidemiology held at the Université de Corse Pascal Paoli in Corsica, France. He has longstanding research and training collaboration with the University of Nairobi in Kenya and has previously been Visiting Faculty on Longitudinal Data Analysis at the Division of Epidemiology and Biostatistics of Stellenbosch University in South Africa. 

Workshop Facilitators

Dr. Wende Clarence Safari is a Tanzanian statistician working with the Inequalities in Cancer Outcomes (ICON) group at the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), UK. Dr. Safari holds a Bachelor’s in Applied Statistics (Mzumbe University, Tanzania), a Master’s in Biostatistics (Hasselt University, Belgium), and a PhD in Statistics (Universidade da Coruña, Spain). She focuses on developing and applying survival analysis methods to optimise the use of electronic health records and patient-reported data. Her research explores inequalities in cancer outcomes by analysing diagnostic pathways. Previously, she worked at NIMR in Mwanza on HIV/AIDS surveillance data and at the Africa Health Research Institute in South Africa, studying access to primary healthcare for individuals with TB using electronic health records.
Innocent B. Mboya is a Senior Technical Advisor at the Africa Academy for Public Health (AAPH), Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. He completed his postdoctoral researcher position in register-based epidemiology at the Department of Translational Medicine, Lund University, Malmö, Sweden. He is also a Lecturer at Kilimanjaro Christian Medical University College, Institute of Public Health, Moshi, Tanzania. Dr. Mboya accumulated over ten years of combined experience as a researcher, project coordinator, and trainer. His experience ranges from public health, epidemiology and applied biostatistics, data quality assurance, monitoring vaccine uptake and coverage (e.g., COVID-19 and childhood vaccines in Tanzania), maternal and child health (investigating predictors of adverse pregnancy outcomes), nutritional status of mothers and children, non-communicable diseases (NCDs, such as, surveys on HPV screening for cervical cancer, hypertension, diabetes, and mental health screening), monitoring HIV infection in key populations, sexual and reproductive health, and healthcare provider workload. He has experience coordinating and conducting community-based surveys, community engagement, and project monitoring and evaluation at the local and national levels. With a background in epidemiology and applied biostatistics, Dr. Mboya possesses diverse data management and analysis skills and has authored several articles and abstracts presented at national and international conferences, demonstrating his track record in scientific writing.
Dr. Neema R. Mosha is working as a medical Statistician and a research associate within the Mwanza Intervention Trials Unit (MITU) and the Institute for Medical Information Processing, Biometry and Epidemiology, Ludwig-Maximilians University, Munich-Germany. She is a qualified Statistician and epidemiologist with over 10 years of experience teaching and conducting research, monitoring and evaluating projects and programs. She has science degree in Statistics and Mathematics from the University of Dodoma (Tanzania), a Master's degree in Epidemiology and applied Bio-statistics from KCMUCo (Tanzania) and PhD in Biostatistics from Stellenbosch University (South-Africa). Dr. Mosha has spent most of her time working with different statistical packages including STATA and R answering research objectives in a wide range of topics such as HIV/AIDS and Sexual Transmitted infections. Non-Communicable Diseases, Gender violence, and women empowerment, Health Systems, Clinical trials and currently Emerging Infectious Diseases. Her major interest is in the application of a wide range of statistical methods in related health research/program to provide insights, recommendations, advice on policy change through the use of the evidence-based approach. Further, she is keen to provide extensive support as a faculty member to the growing arena of epidemiology and biostatistics in sub-Saharan Africa.

Dr. Jacqueline Materu is a Biostatistician and recent PhD graduate in Biostatistics, with over seven years of experience applying advanced statistical methods to health research. Her work focuses on sexual and reproductive health, sexually transmitted infections (HIV and HSV-2), and vulnerable populations, particularly adolescents and young adults. She has collaborated with leading institutions including the National Institute for Medical Research (NIMR) in Tanzania, GTU in the USA, JHPIEGO, INSERM in France, and the Africa Health Research Institute (AHRI) in South Africa, contributing to international research projects across Tanzania, Zambia, South Africa, the United States, and France. Jacqueline has authored multiple peer-reviewed publications and trained researchers and students in statistical methods and public health data analysis. During her PhD, she received prestigious research grants, including from the UK HIV Research Trust and the Early Career Grants Programme from RSTMH in partnership with NIHR, supporting her work on advanced statistical modelling of sexual and reproductive health indicators. Her research interests include longitudinal data analysis, survival analysis, and joint/copula models, with applications to health data.
Dr. Sophia Kagoye is a Research Scientist at the National Institute for Medical Research Mwanza centre. She holds a PhD in epidemiology and applied biostatistics from the CUHAS. Her PhD project topic focused on the trends, patterns and determinants of mortality among older children and adolescents in the Kisesa Health and Demographic Surveillance site.

Jim Todd is a Professor of Applied Biostatistics at CUHAS, Mwanza, Tanzania, emeritus Professor at LSHTM, Senior Technical Advisor, TAZAMA Project, NIMR, Mwanza, and works with several other institutions in Africa. His research interests have focused on several projects that aimed to analyse routinely collected health data. He served as a member of the editorial board of Parasite Immunology, and Tropical Medicine and International Health and a guest editor for Frontiers in Public Health. He is currently a Secretary-Treasurer of the IBS Tanzania Region. In 2018, he received the IBS Rob Kempton Award for his outstanding contributions to the development of biometry in the developing world. He is a co-investigator on the Alpha network, which is funded by Wellcome Trust and the Gates Foundation. He is the PI for the INSPIRE network, which will develop a network for supporting HDSS in East Africa. He has led a project funded by the Global Fund for AIDS, TB and Malaria, which delivered ART in health centres, and monitored the impact on health outcomes.
Dr. Philip Ayieko is an Assistant Professor of Medical Statistics and Epidemiology at LSHTM, and is based fulltime in Mwanza. He has an interest in the design and analysis of clinical trials and has previously designed and analysed cluster randomised trials on child health interventions. Prior to joining the School and the Mwanza Intervention Trials Unit, Philip worked at the Kenya Medical Research Institute-Wellcome Trust programme since 2005 and his areas of work included systematic reviews in child health, costs of treatment for paediatric diseases and cost-effectiveness analysis of vaccination, and quality of hospital care in paediatrics with a focus on pneumonia. Between 2013 and 2017, he served as the lead epidemiologist of the Kenyan clinical information network, a collaborative project between hospitals, Ministry of Health, academic institutions and professional bodies that aims to use hospital data to improve quality of paediatric care.

Dr. Bonny Mgawe is a lecturer at the Nelson Mandela African Institution of Science and Technology (NM-AIST) and a Certified Telecommunication Engineer (Level 3, Registration No. P0656-RFC). He has been involved in several impactful projects. One of his notable collaborations was with King Mongkut’s University of Technology Thonburi (Thailand), where he helped design and develop a device that measures soil health using antennas. This innovation aims to support small-scale farmers by providing a cost-effective alternative to traditional agricultural laboratory testing, thereby promoting modern farming techniques and improving crop yields. Currently, he is actively involved in two national strategic initiatives. The first is the Tanzania Internalisation of Higher Education (TANZIE) project, which seeks to strengthen the international profile and competitiveness of Tanzanian higher education institutions by promoting academic exchange, research collaboration, and institutional capacity building. The second is the Assessment of Artificial Intelligence (AI) Readiness towards the Development of the National AI Strategy in Tanzania, a foundational project aimed at evaluating the country's preparedness for AI adoption and contributing to the formulation of a comprehensive national AI strategy.
